Running a PRISMA Systematic Review for AI in Healthcare Research

Created on June 29, 2026

2026   ·   systematic-review   prisma   research-methods   healthcare-ai   ·   research-methods

Systematic reviews occupy an unusual position in AI research. In a field that moves as fast as machine learning, where a paper can be outdated within a year, it might seem like reviews are a poor fit. In practice, the opposite is often true for applied domains like healthcare AI: individual papers report on individual models, datasets, and metrics, but nobody working in the space has a reliable, unbiased picture of the field as a whole — what’s actually been tried, what consistently works, where the real gaps are — without someone doing the deliberately unglamorous work of systematically collecting and synthesizing that literature.

PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) is the reporting standard that makes this kind of work rigorous and reproducible, rather than an informal literature summary dressed up as a review. This post walks through how the methodology actually works, using two systematic reviews I’ve worked on as concrete examples: a published review of ethical considerations of large language models in healthcare, and a review of LSTM-based deep learning models in medicine that is currently in preparation.

What PRISMA actually is

PRISMA 2020, published by Page and colleagues in the BMJ in 2021 as a full revision of the original 2009 statement, consists of two core components: a 27-item checklist specifying what information a systematic review report needs to include — from the title and abstract through methods, results, and discussion — and a four-phase flow diagram that visually tracks how records move from initial identification through screening, eligibility assessment, and final inclusion. Most health-science journals now require or strongly recommend PRISMA compliance as a condition of publication, which makes understanding the methodology a practical necessity, not just good practice, for anyone doing evidence synthesis in this space.

It’s worth being clear about what PRISMA is not: it’s a reporting standard, not a quality assessment tool in itself, and it doesn’t replace the actual intellectual work of the review — formulating a good research question, searching thoroughly, and synthesizing findings honestly. What it does is force that work to be transparent and auditable, so a reader can trace exactly how you got from “here’s my research question” to “here are my conclusions.”

The process, step by step

1. Define the research question and eligibility criteria before searching. This has to happen first, and it has to be specific enough to guide concrete decisions later. “What are the ethical issues with LLMs in healthcare” is a starting point, not a usable research question — it needs to be narrowed into criteria you can actually apply consistently: which publication years, which study types, which databases, what counts as “healthcare,” and critically, what would make a study ineligible even if it’s topically relevant.

2. Search multiple databases systematically, not just one. A review that searches only one database (even a good one) will systematically miss relevant work, and different fields cluster in different indexing systems. The LSTM review I’ve worked on searches seven databases — Springer Link, ScienceDirect, IEEE Xplore, Wiley, PubMed, ACM Digital Library, and World Scientific — precisely because healthcare AI work is genuinely scattered across both clinical/medical indexes (PubMed) and computer science/engineering indexes (IEEE, ACM), and a review that only checked medical databases would miss a large share of the relevant technical literature, and vice versa.

3. Screen in phases, with pre-registered exclusion criteria. This is where the four-phase flow diagram comes in: records are first identified (total records returned by all searches, before removing duplicates), then screened (typically title and abstract only, against basic eligibility), then assessed for eligibility (full-text review against the complete inclusion criteria), and finally included in the review. Recording the exact count at each phase — and, critically, the reasons records were excluded at the eligibility stage — is what makes the review auditable. In the published ethics review, 316 records were screened across five databases, 42 full texts were assessed for eligibility, and 27 studies were ultimately included at a 3.5-out-of-4 quality threshold. The LSTM review (in preparation) followed a similar structure at larger scale: 329 records screened after de-duplication, filtered against a quality threshold of at least 8.75 out of 10, resulting in a final corpus of 101 studies.

4. Use a quality/risk-of-bias assessment, not just topical relevance. Being about the right topic isn’t sufficient for inclusion — PRISMA reviews typically apply a structured quality threshold, and when more than one reviewer is doing the screening, measuring inter-reviewer agreement (commonly using Cohen’s kappa) demonstrates that the inclusion decisions weren’t arbitrary or idiosyncratic to a single reviewer. The LSTM review used a two-reviewer screening process with an inter-reviewer agreement of κ = 0.82, which is generally interpreted as strong agreement — a number worth reporting precisely because it’s evidence the screening criteria were applied consistently, not just a formality.

5. Extract data systematically, then synthesize — don’t just summarize study-by-study. The final, and often most intellectually demanding, step is moving from a pile of individually-extracted data points to an actual synthesis: identifying patterns, clusters, and gaps across the included studies rather than just describing each one in sequence. The published ethics review identified which ethical themes were most frequently discussed across the 27 included studies (bias and fairness led at 25.9%, followed by safety/reliability, transparency/explainability, and accountability), which model architectures dominated the literature (the GPT family accounted for 51.8% of examined models), and used this synthesis to propose a four-layer ethical integration framework — regulatory safeguards, technical safeguards, human oversight, and transparency/accountability — rather than just listing concerns without structure. The LSTM review similarly maps its 101 included studies across data modality (electronic health records, physiological signals, imaging), common tooling, and identifies six advancement clusters in the literature, including model compression and transfer learning specifically aimed at deployment efficiency — a thread that connects directly to the efficient-AI methods covered elsewhere on this site.

Why this matters more, not less, in a fast-moving field

There’s a reasonable objection to systematic reviews in AI: by the time a review is published, hasn’t the field moved on? For narrow technical benchmarks, this concern has some merit. But for the kind of question systematic reviews are actually good at answering — what ethical risks recur across deployed systems, what architectural patterns are converging on, where the genuine evidence gaps are — the value comes precisely from aggregating across many individual papers rather than trusting any single one, and that kind of synthesis doesn’t go stale as quickly as a single model’s benchmark score does. A well-conducted review of LLM healthcare ethics research is still informative a year later, even as the specific models discussed within it are superseded, because the patterns it identifies — which risks get attention, which get neglected, where the field’s blind spots are — tend to persist longer than any individual system does.

A few practical lessons

  • Decide your eligibility criteria before you see how many results they produce. It’s tempting to loosen criteria after an initial search returns fewer papers than expected, but doing so undermines the whole premise of a pre-specified, reproducible process.
  • Track exclusion reasons at every stage, not just the final count. A reviewer (or reader) questioning why a seemingly relevant paper was excluded should be able to find the specific reason, not just infer it.
  • Report quality thresholds precisely, and apply them consistently. A vague quality bar isn’t really a bar at all — numeric thresholds (a score out of 4, or out of 10) that are applied the same way to every candidate study are what make the “systematic” part of systematic review meaningful rather than aspirational.
  • When you have multiple reviewers, measure and report their agreement. It’s extra work, but it’s one of the clearest signals to a reader that your screening process wasn’t idiosyncratic.

Systematic review methodology isn’t glamorous work, and it’s easy to underrate relative to building a new model or reporting a new benchmark result. But in a field where individual results are noisy, quickly superseded, and unevenly reported, the discipline of PRISMA — search broadly, screen transparently, assess quality explicitly, synthesize honestly — is one of the more durable contributions a researcher can make to a fast-moving area like healthcare AI.
